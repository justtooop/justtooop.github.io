Features of the setup

Feature Explanation of feature
The flow of exhaust gas is opposite to that of the flow of amines. This is known as counter-current flow. This is to maintain a steep diffusion gradient between the concentration of CO2 in the exhaust gas and amine solvents.
The exhaust gas pipe is thin and almost inexpandable. This is to maintain a high partial pressure of carbon dioxide thereby ensuring the rate of diffusion of CO2 into the amine solvents is high.
At the interface to isolate carbon dioxide from the carbamates, the pressure is low and partial pressure of CO2 is kept at almost 0. The following is the reaction that takes place (for secondary and primary amines): NR1R2H (aq) + CO2 (g) ⇌ N(R1R2H)(COO) (aq)
By Le Chatelier's principle, the position of equilibrium will shift to the left and the backward reaction is favoured to increase the number of moles of gas in the system. Hence more CO2 will be produced at the interface.

Feasibility of the reaction

It is commerically viable to use 2-aminoethan-1-ol as the amine solvent. 2-aminoethanol can act as an amine solvent and as a Brønsted-Lowry Base. The activation energy of the reaction is also known to be very low at 7.87 kJ mol-1, showing that the reaction is feasible.

The low activation energy can be attributed to the reaction of CO2 and amine occuring with the aid of base amine. The mechanism of absorption of CO2 by 2-aminoethanol (also known as monoethanolamine or MEA) is explained in this section.

How practical is this application?

A study done by An (2015) has shown that a superhydrophobic ceramic membrane installed in Jinjiang Suyuan Thermal Power Plant (Jinjiang City, China) has been able to continuously remove 90% of CO2. Furthermore, the membrane showed no signs of wearing off after 1 month as it remained clean. The manufacturing of the membrane was similar to that of manufacturing an omniphobic membrane (grafting of ZnO nanoparticles by flourination).
